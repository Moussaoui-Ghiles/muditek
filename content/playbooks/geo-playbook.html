GEO Playbook

Cited, Not Ranked: The Complete GEO Playbook

The page ChatGPT cites and the page Google ranks are usually two different pages. This is the playbook for owning the first one.

Optimize for

Only 6.82% of the pages ChatGPT cites also sit in Google's top 10. Nearly a third of the most-cited AI pages don't rank on Google at all. AI search threw out the old rulebook: it rewards clear structure and reliable sourcing, not backlinks or PageRank, and 83% of AI Overview citations come from pages outside the organic top 10.

That cuts both ways. On queries that surface an AI answer, organic clicks fall ~61%. But the brands cited inside that answer pick up a +35% click lift while everyone else bleeds out. Being cited is the new position #1, and right now roughly 9 of 10 sites have done nothing about it.

This is the complete method, end to end: every step, the proof behind each one, the tactics that waste your time, and the honest limits. The base setup takes about an hour.

Eight moves take existing content from invisible to citable. The first five are the one-hour base. The last three are the advanced knowledge layer.

Part 1: Make Content Readable to AI

1. Sort Crawlers with robots.txt

robots.txt is not an on/off switch. AI crawlers come in five types that do different jobs. Treating them as one bucket either leaks content to training or removes the site from AI search entirely.

Crawler type Examples What it does Recommended
Training GPTBot, ClaudeBot, Meta-ExternalAgent, CCBot Pulls content to train models Block (optional)
Search & retrieval OAI-SearchBot, Claude-SearchBot, PerplexityBot Fetches live to answer queries Allow
User-triggered ChatGPT-User, Claude-User, Perplexity-User, Google-Agent Fires when a user pastes a URL Allow
Opt-out tokens Google-Extended, Applebot-Extended Signals, not crawlers, opt out of training Control
Undeclared Bytespider, xAI Grok bot Do not identify, may ignore rules Block

The rule that matters: blocking search & retrieval crawlers makes the site vanish from AI search. Blocking training crawlers only keeps content out of model training, it does not affect current AI search results.

# Search & retrieval: allow
User-agent: OAI-SearchBot
Allow: /

User-agent: Claude-SearchBot
Allow: /

User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

# User-triggered: allow
User-agent: ChatGPT-User
Allow: /

User-agent: Claude-User
Allow: /

# Training: block
User-agent: GPTBot
Disallow: /

User-agent: CCBot
Disallow: /

# Opt-out tokens
User-agent: Google-Extended
Disallow: /

# Undeclared: block
User-agent: Bytespider
Disallow: /

2. Write an llms.txt

llms.txt is a Markdown file at the site root that describes what the site is, its key pages, and who is behind it, built for AI consumption the way robots.txt is built for crawlers.

Adoption is still low, which is the opportunity. Over 840,000 sites have deployed llms.txt (Anthropic, Cloudflare, Stripe, Vercel among them), yet across ~300,000 domains only 10.13% have one, roughly 9 of 10 sites have not adopted it. Adoption is nearly flat across traffic tiers (8–10%), so being early is a genuine edge.

The format is deliberately simple:

# Your Project Name

> One-line description of what this is.

## Links

- [Documentation](https://yoursite.com/docs)
- [GitHub](https://github.com/you/project)
- [Blog](https://yoursite.com/blog)

## About

Short paragraph explaining the project, its purpose,
key features, and what makes it different.

A filled-in example, which is what AI actually reads:

# Acme Analytics

> Open-source product analytics for developers. Self-hostable, privacy-first, SQL-native.

Acme Analytics is a self-hosted analytics platform built on ClickHouse. It tracks
product events without third-party cookies, exposes raw SQL, and runs on your own
infrastructure. Built for engineering teams that want full data ownership.

## Docs

- [Quickstart](https://acme.dev/docs/quickstart): Install and send your first event in 5 minutes.
- [SQL reference](https://acme.dev/docs/sql): Query event data directly.
- [Self-hosting guide](https://acme.dev/docs/self-host): Deploy with Docker or Kubernetes.

## Product

- [Pricing](https://acme.dev/pricing): Free self-hosted, paid cloud tiers.
- [Acme vs Mixpanel and Amplitude](https://acme.dev/vs): Where Acme fits.

## Optional

- [Changelog](https://acme.dev/changelog): Release history.
- [Blog](https://acme.dev/blog): Engineering write-ups.

One spec detail worth knowing: the H1 (project name) is the only required line; the blockquote summary and section lists are recommended. A section titled ## Optional carries special meaning, crawlers may skip those links when a shorter context is needed, so reserve it for secondary material.

A real, well-structured llms.txt reads like a clean machine-readable profile, identity, core projects, quick recommendations, links:

After publishing, submit it to directory.llmstxt.cloud, llmstxt.site, and the llms-txt-hub repository on GitHub via PR. Changes take effect after crawlers revisit, usually within a few days.

3. Cross-Link Sites into a Discovery Mesh

For multiple properties, have each site's llms.txt link to the others. A crawler entering through any one site can follow the links and discover everything. One entry point exposes the whole network.

Add the sibling sites as a section inside every property's llms.txt:

## Network

- [Main site](https://example.com/llms.txt): Product, docs, and pricing.
- [Blog](https://blog.example.com/llms.txt): Engineering write-ups and guides.
- [Changelog](https://changelog.example.com/llms.txt): Release history.

Every property lists every other one. A crawler entering through any single site follows the links and reaches the whole network.

4. Serve Full Content and Markdown Routes

llms.txt is the summary. llms-full.txt is the complete version, typically 30–60KB, containing project descriptions, FAQs, usage scenarios, competitive comparisons, and README excerpts.

A workable llms-full.txt skeleton:

# Acme Analytics, Full Knowledge Base

> Open-source, self-hosted product analytics. Privacy-first, SQL-native.

## What it is
[Two or three paragraphs: the problem, the approach, who it is for.]

## Core features
[Bulleted, each with one line of context.]

## How it compares
[Acme vs Mixpanel, Amplitude, PostHog: one honest line each.]

## Use cases
[Concrete scenarios, phrased the way users actually ask.]

## FAQ
[Real questions, direct answers.]

## Install
[The exact commands.]

## Links
[Docs, GitHub, pricing, blog.]

The full version gets far more traffic. In a 7-day analysis of CDN logs, llms-full.txt was visited at a median of 79 vs 14 for llms.txt (mean 248 vs 62), roughly 4–5x more, with ChatGPT the majority of visits. The reason: LLMs tend to embed full content upfront rather than retrieving page by page. AI that finds the summary wants the full version.

Markdown routes go one step further. Serving a .md version of every page strips the noise: a 15,000-token HTML page becomes a ~3,000-token Markdown document, about 80% less to parse.

Declare the Markdown alternate in the page <head>:

<link rel="alternate" type="text/markdown" href="/page.md" />

Claude Code and Cursor already send Accept: text/markdown headers when fetching docs. This is standard HTTP/1.1 content negotiation, in the spec since 1997.

Part 2: Make Sure AI Can Find You

Readable content is useless if AI never reaches it. AI search does not run its own web crawl from scratch, it leans on existing search indexes:

ChatGPT searchruns on Bing
Google AI OverviewsuseGoogle's own index
Perplexityrelies onexternal search APIs

If pages are not indexed by Google and Bing, none of the Part 1 work matters.

5. Register with Google

  1. Open Google Search Console, verify the domain (DNS record or HTML file).
  2. Submit the sitemap (yoursite.com/sitemap.xml).
  3. Check the Pages report for indexed vs excluded pages.
  4. For any important page not indexed, use URL Inspection → Request Indexing.

Google has no AI-specific panel yet, but AI Overviews pull from a wider range than the top 10, even pages that do not rank can appear in AI answers.

6. Register with Bing, and Turn On IndexNow

Bing matters more than its market share suggests: Copilot, DuckDuckGo, Yahoo, and ChatGPT search all run on the Bing index underneath.

  1. Register in Bing Webmaster Tools, submit the sitemap.
  2. Check the AI Performance panel to see how often AI cites the content.
  3. Set up IndexNow so Bing is notified the moment new content publishes, instead of waiting for a crawl.

IndexNow setup:

  • Generate a key: 8–128 characters, hex only (a–z, A–Z, 0–9, dashes). Host it as {key}.txt at the site root to prove ownership.
  • On publish, POST to api.indexnow.org/indexnow with host, key, and a urlList of changed URLs (up to 10,000 per request); a single URL can also go via GET. An HTTP 200 means received.
  • Bing picks them up within minutes, and one submission propagates to every IndexNow engine. Most static-site generators and CMS platforms have IndexNow plugins.

The key file, hosted at your root, contains nothing but the key string:

https://example.com/a1b2c3d4e5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5d6.txt

Ping a single changed URL with a GET request:

https://api.indexnow.org/indexnow?url=https://example.com/new-page&key=a1b2c3d4e5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5d6

Or submit up to 10,000 URLs at once with a POST:

{
  "host": "example.com",
  "key": "a1b2c3d4e5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5d6",
  "keyLocation": "https://example.com/a1b2c3d4e5f6a7b8c9d0e1f2a3b4c5d6.txt",
  "urlList": [
    "https://example.com/new-page",
    "https://example.com/updated-page"
  ]
}

An HTTP 200 means the request was received. One ping reaches every IndexNow engine.

Part 3: Build a Dedicated Knowledge Site (Advanced)

Instead of letting AI scrape scattered sources, give it a single, organized entry point. This is the highest-leverage move for anyone with multiple products or a body of work.

8. Three Layers + a Structured API

A knowledge site provides three layers, plus machine-readable data:

  1. Overview, llms.txt.
  2. Full version, llms-full.txt (30–60KB).
  3. Standalone knowledge pages, one per core project.
  4. JSON APIs, so AI tools can fetch data programmatically.

Pull data live from upstream (e.g. the GitHub API) with caching that refreshes periodically, keeping maintenance near zero.

Give AI a narrative, not a list. When a model answers "who is this" or "what does this team do," a coherent description that connects the projects, how they relate, the technical direction, the overall picture, works far better than a flat list.

A reference implementation serves an llms.txt overview, a 50KB llms-full.txt, per-project pages, and JSON endpoints (/api/profile, /api/projects, /api/blog, /api/weekly) that pull live GitHub data with hourly ISR caching. 9. Give Each Project Its Own Page

Each project needs a standalone, self-contained Markdown page, not a row in a list. Include a citable summary, core features, competitive comparisons, use cases, and install commands.

Copy this template per project. It mirrors the structure that actually gets cited:

Note what earns the citation: a one-line citable summary at the top, a real comparison, use cases phrased the way people ask them, and copy-paste install commands.

URL structure is a ranking signal. Natural-language slugs get cited more than opaque IDs. /projects/pake tells the model what the page is about before it reads a word; /page?id=47 tells it nothing. Cited pages have titles with noticeably higher semantic similarity to the user's query, so the title and slug should mirror how people actually ask.

10. Mirror Structured Data to the Main Domain

Subdomains carry less authority than root domains, and a crawler that discovers example.com will not automatically find docs.example.com or api.example.com. Data spread across subdomains means AI sees only part of the picture.

The fix: mirror the key structured data onto the main domain so example.com/llms.txt, example.com/projects/xxx.md, and example.com/api/projects.json all live under one roof. Crawlers find the main site through search indexing and reach everything without leaving.

Implementation options: scheduled CI sync, build-time fetching, or reverse proxy. A reference setup uses a GitHub Action that syncs subdomain data to the main repo nightly.

Launch Checklist

For every new site, run this list to avoid gaps:

  • robots.txt, crawler permissions sorted by type
  • llms.txt, site summary with cross-references to other properties
  • sitemap, submitted to Google and Bing
  • Bing Webmaster Tools, IndexNow enabled
  • Google Search Console, indexing monitored
  • Each property's llms.txt references the others (discovery mesh)

The biggest trap is chasing every GEO technique at once, creating a mess and losing the plot. The base five steps deliver most of the value.

Part 4: What Actually Moves Visibility

GEO is not folklore. The numbers below come from controlled testing of what changes citation, and they converge.

Which Content Changes Lift Visibility

Nine content modifications were tested against a 10,000-query benchmark across nine domains, then validated on a live engine.

The numbers, stated precisely. The chart headlines +115% / +43% / +33%. The per-method figures are more specific, and the distinction matters for setting expectations:

  • The three strongest methods, Cite Sources, Quotation Addition, Statistics Addition, each lift visibility +30–40% (position-adjusted metric; +15–30% on the subjective metric). The methods that add credible substance win.
  • The +115% figure is not a blanket "authoritative" lift. It is the specific result for Cite Sources applied to a page ranked #5 in search (+115.1%), the democratization finding below. The "Authoritative tone" method on its own measured roughly +89%.
  • The classic SEO move, Keyword Stuffing, does not work. On the live Perplexity test it scored 10% worse than doing nothing.

All nine modifications tested, ordered by effect:

  1. Cite Sources, add citations to credible sources (top tier)
  2. Quotation Addition, add direct quotes from authorities (top tier)
  3. Statistics Addition, replace vague claims with hard numbers (top tier)
  4. Fluency Optimization, tighten and clarify the writing (strong, and the best combiner)
  5. Authoritative tone, write with confidence and authority (~+89%)
  6. Easy-to-Understand, simplify language and structure
  7. Unique Words, add distinctive, specific terminology
  8. Technical Terms, add precise domain vocabulary
  9. Keyword Stuffing, the old SEO reflex: fails, roughly 10% worse than baseline

Methods 1 to 3 add credible substance and win across the board. Methods 4 to 8 sharpen content that already exists. Method 9 is the one to drop.

How visibility is measured (so the numbers above mean something): two metrics. Position-Adjusted Word Count is the share of the answer's words that cite you, weighted higher when your citation lands earlier. Subjective Impression scores seven things an LLM judges, relevance, influence, uniqueness, position, amount of your content shown, click-likelihood, and diversity.

Method effectiveness varies by content type (use this to pick a strategy):

Method Best-fit domains
Authoritative tone Debate, History, Science
Fluency Optimization Business, Science, Health
Cite Sources Statements / Facts, Law & Government
Quotation Addition People & Society, Explanation, History
Statistics Addition Law & Government, Debate, Opinion

GEO democratizes visibility. Because generative engines condition on content rather than backlinks, lower-ranked sites gain the most. Cite Sources gave a site ranked #5 in search a +115.1% visibility increase, while the top-ranked site lost 30.3%. Small creators can compete on content quality alone.

Combine methods. The strongest pair is Fluency Optimization + Statistics Addition (more than 5.5% above any single method). Cite Sources is weak alone but the best combiner (+31.4% on average when paired).

It generalizes to real engines. On Perplexity, Quotation Addition lifted visibility +22%, and Statistics Addition reached +37% on the subjective metric.

What Gets a Page Cited

Running 602 prompts across the three major AI platforms and analyzing tens of thousands of pages surfaces the patterns most useful for creators:

  • Specificity wins. Pages with real data, clear definitions, and side-by-side comparisons see 50%+ higher impact than vague pages. Step-by-step structure helps. Pure FAQ format actively hurts, the opposite of what most "GEO score" tools advise.
  • Depth wins. AI favors long content it can slice into reusable segments. High-impact pages average ~2,000 words with 10+ headings; low-impact pages average ~170 words, a 10x gap. Sweet spot: 1,000–3,000 words.
  • Relevance beats mechanics. Heading hierarchy, meta descriptions, and keyword density predict less than one variable: whether the page actually answers the question asked.
  • Platforms differ. ChatGPT cites few sources but uses each deeply, per-citation impact over 5x Google's, so go deep per page. Perplexity casts a wider net, citing 2x+ as many sources, so go wide.
  • Content type matters. Official sites, news, and industry verticals are ~80% of citation sources, but encyclopedia and explainer pages carry 3x the impact of news pages. 83% of citations are English content, international projects need English versions.

How Citation Actually Works

  • Retrieved ≠ cited. Of all pages ChatGPT retrieves in a session, only 15% make the final answer. Getting into the retrieval pool is just the first hurdle.
  • Third-party beats first-party. Brands are cited 6.5x more through third-party sources (Reddit, Hacker News) than through their own domain. A well-structured llms.txt still matters: it gives the model a citable anchor to point to when the triggering conversation happened elsewhere.
  • Front-load the value. 44.2% of LLM citations come from the first 30% of the text; pages with claim-rich introductions are cited 2.1x more often.
  • Length compounds. Pages over 20,000 characters average 10.18 citations each vs 2.39 for pages under 500, a 4.3x multiplier. Comprehensive guides are cited 3–5x more than thin content.
  • Structure is read. 68.7% of ChatGPT citations follow a logical H1→H2→H3 hierarchy; 61% of cited pages use structured data markup.
  • Concrete data outperforms. Product pages with benchmark data (pricing comparisons, performance metrics) are cited 2.8x more than generic descriptions.
  • Rank still nudges, but loosely. A #1 organic result has roughly a 33% chance of being cited; #10 still has about 13%. The curve is shallow, which is exactly why off-page-one content wins so often.

Part 5: What Does NOT Work

Equally important: the techniques that waste time or backfire.

Tactic Verdict
<meta name="ai-content-url">, <meta name="llms"> No spec, no adoption by any major AI system. Noise.
/.well-known/ai.txt Competing proposals, no real adoption. Wait for a winner.
HTML comments with "AI hints" Parsers strip comments before the AI sees content.
User-Agent sniffing to serve Markdown to bots This is cloaking. Google penalizes it.
Unofficial AI meta tags Unless a major provider documents support, it is noise.
Adding FAQ sections to "boost a score" The data shows pure FAQ format hurts.
Keyword stuffing Measured performing worse than no optimization at all.

The JSON-LD Reality

JSON-LD seems like it should help AI visibility. The evidence says otherwise. When data is placed only in JSON-LD and not shown on the visible page, all five major AI systems fail to find it. LLMs treat <script type="application/ld+json"> as plain text: they read the words inside but ignore the structured semantics.

The one exception is Bing/Copilot, which uses JSON-LD to enrich its index. Keep existing JSON-LD (it helps Bing/Copilot and traditional rich results), but do not add it expecting ChatGPT or Claude to cite more because of it.

What to keep, since it helps Bing/Copilot and rich results and costs nothing elsewhere:

<script type="application/ld+json">
{
  "@context": "https://schema.org",
  "@type": "SoftwareApplication",
  "name": "Acme Analytics",
  "description": "Self-hosted, privacy-first product analytics.",
  "offers": { "@type": "Offer", "price": "0", "priceCurrency": "USD" }
}
</script>

The rule: mirror anything important in visible page text too, because ChatGPT and Claude read the words, not the schema.

Part 6: Trade-offs and Reality Check

An honest playbook states the limits.

1. llms.txt has no proven citation lift yet. In controlled testing, having an llms.txt file did not make a domain more likely to be cited; the model performed slightly better without it. The honest position: llms.txt is a low-cost, early-mover signal that makes content cleaner to parse, but there is no measured citation guarantee today. Deploy it because it is cheap and forward-looking, not because it is proven to lift citations.

2. AI citation attribution is unreliable. Eight AI search tools were tested on 200 article-attribution queries.

Collectively they answered over 60% incorrectly, Perplexity 37%, Grok-3 94%. Worse, they were confidently wrong: ChatGPT signaled doubt only 15 times in 200 and never declined to answer; DeepSeek misattributed the source 115 times; over half of Grok-3 and Gemini answers linked to fabricated or broken URLs. The paid tiers (Perplexity Pro, Grok-3) were more confidently wrong, not less. Do the structural work because it makes content easier to access accurately, but never treat an AI citation as proof a user saw the exact words. The mechanism is still maturing.

Tool Error rate and behavior
Perplexity 37% of queries answered wrong
Grok-3 94% wrong; 154 of 200 citations led to dead URLs
ChatGPT 134 of 200 wrong; flagged doubt only 15 times, never declined
DeepSeek misattributed the source 115 times
Copilot the cautious exception: declined more often than it answered
Paid tiers (Perplexity Pro, Grok-3) more confidently wrong than the free versions

3. It is brand visibility, not traffic. AI search is still under 1% of referral traffic. The payoff is being present and described accurately when AI answers questions in the space, not a flood of clicks.

4. Do not let scores drive decisions. Audit tools that tell you to add FAQ sections, trust pages, or more text optimize for a score, not for citations. The only test that matters: does every paragraph add information not already on the page? If not, cut it. Padding to raise a score is the failure mode.

What Success Looks Like

When the structure is in place, AI answers questions about the work accurately and points to the right pages, even for niche, specific queries.

The One-Hour Implementation Order

  1. robots.txt, allow search & retrieval crawlers, block training/undeclared.
  2. llms.txt, at the root, with the simple format; cross-link any sibling sites.
  3. llms-full.txt + .md routes, full content, low noise, declared in <head>.
  4. Google Search Console + Bing Webmaster Tools, verify, submit sitemap, enable IndexNow.
  5. Perplexity publisher program, if eligible.

Then, when time allows: the knowledge endpoint (Part 3), per-project pages, and the nightly mirror to the main domain.

Give crawlers a few days to re-index, then test by searching the project or brand name in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude. The citations and descriptions should be more accurate.
