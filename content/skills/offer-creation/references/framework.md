# Offer Framework

## Framework sequencing

Apply three frameworks in order. Each builds on the previous:

1. **Hormozi (what to build):** Value equation, dream outcome, perceived likelihood, guarantees, bonus stacking, pricing from value. This gives you the offer skeleton — what it promises and why the math works.
2. **Haynes (how to frame it):** "You're f\*\*\*ing me" scale, costly not expensive, desire density for premium buyers, 30-day recurring value test, tier ladder. This pressure-tests whether the offer feels absurdly good at every phase.
3. **Fazio (how to deliver it):** Fulfillment factory, client intelligence extraction, pre-call consumption sequence, communication cadence, case study flywheel, 80/20 acquisition-to-fulfillment ratio. This operationalizes the offer so you know exactly how to deliver it before you sell it.

Do not skip step 3. An offer that looks good on paper but has no operational blueprint is not a real offer.

## Core model
Build offers by increasing perceived value and realized outcomes while reducing buyer risk, delay, and effort.

Design every offer across 6 parts:
1. Positioning — who it is for and why now
2. Packaging — what is included
3. Pricing — how value is captured
4. Qualification — who should and should not enter
5. Fulfillment — how outcomes are delivered repeatedly
6. Retention / ascension — how value compounds over time

Additional rule: if the offer is meant for scale, it should be sellable through cold traffic or outbound without depending only on referrals.

## Value equation
Use this as the main design lens:

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

### Increase value by raising:
- Dream outcome: make the end state concrete, important, and economically meaningful
- Perceived likelihood: add proof, specificity, authority, transparent mechanism, and credibility

### Increase value by lowering:
- Time delay: shorten time to first win and time to core outcome
- Effort and sacrifice: reduce buyer workload, decisions, confusion, and operational burden

## "You're f\*\*\*ing me" scale

At every phase of the engagement, the value must flip so far in the buyer's favor that a reasonable person would say "you're f\*\*\*ing me, this is too good." This is the litmus test for whether the offer is structured right.

Check the scale at each phase independently:
- Entry / diagnostic phase: what they pay vs what they receive. The output of the diagnostic alone should be worth multiples of the price.
- Build / implementation phase: what they pay vs what the outcome eliminates or creates. Compare to the cost of alternatives (Big Four, enterprise SaaS, hiring in-house).
- Retainer / ongoing phase: what they pay monthly vs what would happen if they stopped. The cost of NOT having the retainer should dwarf the retainer price.

If the scale does not flip at any phase, restructure that phase before selling the offer.

## Costly not expensive

Reframe price as investment by making inaction more expensive than action. Show the math explicitly:
- What does NOT buying cost them? (lost revenue, risk exposure, wasted time, missed opportunity)
- What does buying cost them? (your price)
- The gap between those two numbers is the value. That gap should be absurd.

"Costly" means the number is large. "Not expensive" means relative to what they get or what they lose, it's a bargain. Always calculate and present both sides.

## Desire stacking
For premium or high-ticket offers, combine multiple compatible desires into one coherent promise.

Common desires:
- More revenue / cash flow
- Time leverage / freedom
- Status / positioning
- Convenience / quality
- Risk reduction / wealth protection
- Health / energy / longevity when relevant

Rule: lead with the strongest desired outcome, then layer secondary outcomes that increase attractiveness without making the promise messy.

Desire density for premium buyers: rich buyers do not buy on price. They buy on desire density — how many things they want that your offer gives them simultaneously. For premium offers, stack 3-5 desires where each desire is independently compelling. The more desires the offer satisfies at once, the more irresistible it becomes regardless of price.

## Deliverable stacking
Make the offer concrete. Every deliverable should remove a specific buyer constraint.

Useful deliverable categories:
- Diagnostic / strategy
- Templates / scripts / assets
- Setup / implementation
- DFY or DWY execution support
- Feedback / accountability
- Troubleshooting / escalation
- Reporting / optimization

Rule: do not add random bonuses. Add only components that improve certainty, speed, or ease.
Also document the core process clearly enough that it can be repeated, measured, and improved.

## Tiering
Use these tiers when appropriate:
- DIY — lowest involvement, lowest certainty, slowest path
- DWY — guided implementation, medium certainty, medium speed
- DFY — highest involvement from operator, highest certainty, fastest path

Rule: higher tiers must improve speed and certainty, not just contain more items.

## Guarantees and risk reversal
Use guarantees to reduce buyer fear, especially when proof is limited.

Main types:
- Unconditional guarantee
- Conditional guarantee
- Performance-based guarantee
- Strong-positioning / anti-guarantee when authority is already high

Design rules:
- Match the guarantee to what can actually be controlled
- State eligibility and client responsibilities clearly
- Avoid moral hazard
- Use stronger risk reversal earlier; reduce reliance on it as authority and proof grow

## Pricing logic
Price from value, certainty, and economics — not from competitor averages alone.

Rules:
- High price is acceptable when expected ROI or utility is clearly high
- Price can filter for better-fit, more committed buyers
- Start where delivery certainty is high
- Raise price as systems, proof, and speed-to-result improve
- Reverse-engineer economics using close rate, show rate, volume, channel cost, and fulfillment capacity
- Prefer collection structures that improve commitment and cash flow when justified
- Use price and structure in ways that support later specialization of marketing, sales, and fulfillment if the offer scales

## Recurring value
Recurring pricing requires fresh value before the next rebill.

Recurring value can come from:
- Ongoing optimization
- New assets / updates
- Support / accountability
- New wins unlocked over time
- Continuous implementation help

Hard test (30-day recurring value test): every month, the client must be able to point to what they received THIS month that is worth more than what they paid THIS month. If they cannot, the retainer is weak and they will cancel. This is not a principle — it is a concrete test. Apply it literally to every recurring offer by naming the specific monthly deliverable and confirming its standalone value exceeds the monthly price.

Retention and churn are the real proof of recurring offer quality.

## Premium buyer psychology
Premium buyers care about:
- Time saved
- Certainty
- Quality
- Trust
- Ease of decision

Implications:
- Be direct and concise
- Show fit and investment range early when useful
- Use stronger proof and transparent mechanics
- Reduce decision friction
- Use urgency or incentives only when justified, never as cheap gimmicks
- Customer experience and professionalism matter; premium buyers judge execution quality before purchase and after purchase

Useful premium funnel shapes:
- Lead form plus rapid follow-up / text qualification
- Short VSL plus call booking

Rule: the best funnel is the one that reduces friction while preserving qualification quality.

## Qualification
Qualification is part of offer design, not just sales ops.

Filter for:
- Problem severity
- Urgency
- Financial capacity
- Ability / willingness to implement
- Decision authority
- Channel and delivery fit
- Timeline fit

Qualification layers:
- Front-end pre-qualification in the message, page, or ad
- Application qualification through fit questions and disqualifiers
- Human qualification through call, text, or manual review

Calendar and pre-call rules:
- Wider calendar availability can improve show rate, especially early stage
- Reduce unnecessary tech friction in booking flows
- Use pre-call confirmation, reminders, and expectation-setting
- Optimize speed-to-call after lead capture whenever possible

Rule: qualification should improve quality without adding needless friction.

## Scoring rubric
Evaluate every offer on:
- Outcome strength
- Certainty strength
- Speed to result
- Buyer effort burden
- Risk posture and guarantee fit
- Delivery feasibility
- Margin sustainability
- Recurring defensibility
- Market fit / willingness to pay
- Qualification clarity
- Unit economics
- Cold-traffic or outbound fit when relevant

Do not greenlight offers that score poorly on delivery feasibility, certainty, or economics, even if they sound exciting.

## Fulfillment factory

Every step of delivery must be mapped to a station before the offer is considered complete. If you cannot map the stations, you cannot deliver the offer. Do not sell what you cannot operationalize.

Each station must specify:
- Station number and name
- What happens at this station
- Who does it: you / AI / automation / partner / client
- How long it takes
- Dependencies on previous stations

Map a separate fulfillment factory for each phase of the offer (diagnostic, build, retainer). If the offer has tiers (DIY/DWY/DFY), map stations for each tier.

Design rules:
- Push repeatable mindless work toward AI or automation
- Keep mindful work (strategy, review, client-facing) with you
- Every station should have a clear input and output
- If a station takes too long, split it or automate part of it
- The factory is how you know your capacity — how many clients you can serve simultaneously

## 80/20 acquisition to fulfillment ratio

Spend 80% of energy on acquisition (offer design, positioning, proof, outreach) and 20% on fulfillment. The fulfillment factory exists so that delivery is systematized enough that it does not eat all your time. If fulfillment is consuming you, the factory is not built right — go back and automate or eliminate stations.

## Client intelligence extraction

Use structured intake forms to extract everything needed to deliver before starting work. The intake replaces discovery calls and back-and-forth. The form does the thinking for the client — they just answer.

Design rules:
- 20-30 questions, structured by section
- Every question must tie to a delivery station — if no station needs the answer, remove the question
- Sections should follow a logical order: basics, current state, problem specifics, access/contacts
- The form should take 20-30 minutes to complete
- Send immediately after payment or commitment — this is the first step of delivery

The intake form is not optional. It is the foundation of the fulfillment factory. Without it, you are guessing what the client needs and asking questions piecemeal throughout delivery.

## Pre-call consumption sequence

Between booking and the sales call, deliver 3 touchpoints. The purpose is that by the time they show up on the call, they are already sold. Consumption equals money.

Touchpoint 1 (immediately after booking): Confirmation email plus short video (3-5 minutes). Explain what happens on the call, what you will cover, and what to have ready. Show a sample deliverable or template — honest, not fake data. Set expectations.

Touchpoint 2 (day 2 after booking): One specific teaching insight. A stat, a case study, a regulatory fact, a market insight — something that positions you as the person who knows the domain. Include the source. This is not a pitch. It is proof of expertise delivered for free.

Touchpoint 3 (day before the call): Reminder plus guarantee plus urgency. Restate the guarantee, remind them of the deadline or cost of waiting, confirm the time. Short — 3-4 sentences.

After you have case studies: replace touchpoint 2 with a real case study.

Design rules:
- All 3 touchpoints should be automated (email sequences)
- Match the language and tone to the buyer (formal for enterprise/German markets, direct for SMB)
- The sequence should feel valuable, not salesy
- If someone consumes all 3 touchpoints and shows up, the close rate will be significantly higher than cold calls

## Communication cadence

Every engagement phase has its own async update rhythm. The client should never have to ask "what is happening?" If they ask, the cadence failed.

Design rules:
- Define the cadence per phase (diagnostic, build, retainer)
- Specify what communication happens, when, and through what channel
- Do not tell the client about the cadence. Just do it. Consistent, professional, predictable.
- Updates should be short and concrete — what was done, what is next, what (if anything) is needed from them
- No communication during working phases is acceptable only if the client was told when to expect the next update

Typical patterns:
- Diagnostic: update at intake receipt, after interviews, at delivery
- Build: update every 1-2 weeks with progress, at each milestone, at handoff
- Retainer: monthly report, as-needed notifications, quarterly call

## Case study flywheel

Build case study production into delivery, not after it. Every client engagement should produce a case study by default.

Production steps built into delivery:
- At intake: capture "before" state — current metrics, pain, gaps, exposure
- At delivery: capture "after" state — what was delivered, what changed, results
- At 90 days post-delivery: follow up on outcomes, get updated metrics
- At delivery or 90 days: ask for permission to use anonymized results as a case study

Case study template:
- Industry / company size
- Problem before engagement
- What was delivered
- Timeline
- Results / outcomes after
- Anonymize by default unless client agrees to named study

Flywheel: client becomes case study. Case study becomes content (pre-call sequences, LinkedIn, website, guest articles). Content attracts new clients. New clients become case studies. The flywheel compounds over time.

Rule: if you complete an engagement and do not produce a case study, you wasted a flywheel cycle. Track case study production as a delivery metric.
